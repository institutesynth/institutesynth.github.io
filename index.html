Personal Statement

I'm basically trying to say this is the field I want to study. I hope it's convincing enough.

The smooth cap of a ballpoint pen moves circularly around my arms, then shifts up and does the same circular motion. The man with the pen, a physician freshly off his shift from a major hospital in Saigon, sat knee to knee with me in his makeshift clinic on the first floor of his home, where moped parking meets Buddhist tributes, and medical inquiries were welcomed. Here, expertise flowed as freely as the traffic outside, for a nominal fee or none at all. As he demonstrated the distinctive pattern a parasite would leave behind if it were moving in my body, he explained that the mysterious red bumps on my arms lacked this pattern, suggesting they were most likely hives instead. Prior to this evaluation, back across in the world in America, I saw 2 specialists, had no allergy tests done, and was only told to take Zyrtec everyday. My dad's plea for a prescription of Phenergan fell on deaf ears, and they rushed us out of the room. Even though the origins of the hives remained elusive in Vietnam, the physician's willingness to share medical knowledge beyond the confines of the hospital and his close and genuine care remains etched in my memory, marking the first time I seriously considered medicine, and a breakaway from the profit-driven evaluations I was familiar with.

Shortly after arriving back to America, I was met with dread about the reality of finding a viable career path after college, when none of the jobs at the career fairs ignited any drive or flame within me. However, a wizard of computers, Professor Foley, taught my data structures class, always springing into action and writing a few lines of code to get the computer to follow his every whim whenever a student needed help with an assignment, whether it was making a linked list, or a loop for the snake game, or in my case, rewriting a whole code file to make a graph that took in two parameters. Other students praised him for being able to code anything, and seeing it time and time again, as well as his swift, yet crystal clear responses to student questions on Piazza, along his willingness to schedule meetings outside of his office hours, I wanted to be just like him: an expert in something with the heart of compassion and advocacy.

I continued down the path of computer science by taking an offer for the REU In Data Science program at Harvey Mudd College, hoping that I would walk away with a research topic that I'd be passionate enough to pursue for graduate school. At this summer program, I was standing at the intersection between mathematics, biology, and personalized medicine, integrating both science and technology, contributing to the field of Type I diabetes to help afflicted patients live longer and healthier by developing a mathematical model to create more personalized vaccine and therapeutic solutions than the current one-size-fits-all treatments for the life-long ailment. However, deep down, I wanted to do more than enhance lives by writing code in front of a laptop. I wanted to see and make connections with the data points on my computer screen, to touch lives directly.

My entry into patient care started in the hospital's 'blackbox,' known as the BWH Clinical Microbiology Lab, where patient samples came in, and results came out. There, I performed and interpreted critical microbiology tests such as blood cultures, acting as an unseen force that guided physicians on the next steps in the patient's care. While each sample narrated a tale of the patient's affliction and suffering, it was the same problem I had at Harvey Mudd: I yearned for greater involvement in patient care beyond mere samples and data points on the computer. Before long, I found myself a floor above my usual workspace, seated alongside Dr. Sholl and her trainees at the 8-headed microscope, which was exclusively reserved for pulmonary pathology signouts. We went through folder after folder, examining slide after slide tinted in shades of pink and purple. As we examined a left lung biopsy, a slide with a striking pink and purple circular morphology appeared. Suspecting a fungus ball, Dr Sholl had her fellow pull up the patient's chest X-Ray. She asked me if I knew how to read the film, and I admitted that I did not. However, she jumped up and explained how to read the opacities and pointed out all the abnormalities. I've seen this enthusiasm to explain in a crystal clear manner before, in Professor Foley always going into action diagnosing computer science issues, and in the doctor explaining parasitic infections back in Vietnam. At that moment, I realized that a physician was not just a healer, but also an educator. They mastered their craft, but they wanted to share it with the world and inspire new students to take the same path. I saw a future studying medicine and teaching it, and I wanted to explore it even more.

To plunge into the heart of medicine, I became a medical scribe, witnessing patient-physician interactions first-hand. Early on, I found myself struggling to keep up when physicians spoke Spanish, a language I had never studied. Determined to bridge this gap, I spent my free time to learning, and soon, I understood most conversations and was able to see native speakers' faces light up when I ask them questions in their language. Eventually, I helped translate for Vietnamese-speaking patients. A memorable encounter involved a Vietnamese-only speaking man who struggled with alcohol dependence and came to the ED every week intoxicated. When I translated for the patient, he shared his earnest desire to quit drinking. The depth of his plea, evident as our eyes locked and speaking in a defeated tone, might have been lost if a phone translator had been used. I never saw him ever again after that night, but the experience serves as a reminder of the crucial role of direct communication in healthcare. Watching a shared languages connect people cemented my drive to advocate for immigrant communities, ensuring they receive care that acknowledges not just their symptoms, but their struggles, identities, and stories.

On the other side of medicine, rather than observing stabilizing patients or life-saving treatments, I helped individuals navigate their final months through hospice volunteering. Inspired by the warmth and skills of physicians I observed, I learned to tailor my approach to meet each patient's unique needs. A patient who will always stay with me is "Shirley." We bonded over our love for rough collies, as she used to have one named Teddy. Recognizing an opportunity to bring her some joy, I started bringing my rough collie along to my visits. They both adored each other instantly, with my dog often resting in besides Shirley, and Shirley feeding her. When I did visited alone, Shirley would always ask for her - a reminder to how small gestures could profoundly touch a patient's final days. As her condition worsened, with recurrent UTIs leaving her to more frail and confused every week, I was reminded of the bitter-sweet nature of hospice care: forming deep connections with patients, yet knowing their time is limited. Despite the heartache of seeing friends pass away, hospice is not a failure or a limitation of medicine, but a profound act of human expression. I connected with patients through speaking with them and their families, absorbing their life story, and ensuring they felt heard, valued, and at peace in their final moments.

Shadowing dedicated physicians, enduring unpredictable nights scribing in the ED, and connecting with hospice patients transformed a simple curiosity into an active pursuit. I came to realize that medicine is more than just science: it is a human art that blends technical expertise with deep empathy, where each patient's story can fundamentally shift the approach to care. Through the guidance of my mentors, I am committed to embracing both the rigors and rewards of medicine, continously learning, innovating, and enriching the lives of my future patients and colleagues.

  • September 2016 → May 2019

Medical Scribe

With the caffeine wearing off and the sun about to set, the doctor and I walked down the long corridors of the ED to see our last patient for the overnight shift. He came in for a psychiatric evaluation after his mother called the police on him. During the visit, the patient chronicled the many hardships that led him to the ED, from losing his job, to being kicked out of a homeless shelter. Not once did the doctor interrupt him or attempt to cut his story short. The interaction showed me that medicine isn't just about treating the disease, but also about humanity. When I was a patient, whether for a routine annual checkup or specialist visit, I often felt rushed and dismissed. These negative experiences distorted my view of medicine, where patients are just another paycheck for the doctors. However, the thorough and caring doctors I scribed for changed my jaded view of medicine from profit-driven care to patient-centric care. Through this patient interaction, I saw the type of doctor I wanted to be: one who listens and connects with patients and works with them to achieve their health goals by accounting for any factors which would limit their path.

Clinical Research Coordinator II

At MGH Biostatistics, I noticed a fragmented atmosphere with missed opportunities for collaboration and camaraderie, evidenced by little interactions except for a "hi" exchanged in the hallways. After 6 months devoid of any team-building events, spurred by my colleagues' persistent encouragement, I took the initiative to organize a group dinner. That night, as I bonded with a coworker over our mutual passion for Skyrim and Mass Effect, I realized how personal connections could transcend professional boundaries and transform a working environment. As the department began to stick together like glue, I saw firsthand how fostering a cohesive team directly affected the department's output and continuity. We retained more staff, preserved invaluable institutional knowledge, and improved our overall efficiency. This experience cemented my belief in the power of interpersonal connections within professional medical settings. Effective patient care hinges not just on the competence of individual healthcare providers, but on their ability to communicate and collaborate seamlessly. Moving forward, I am committed to fostering strong, interconnected teams, ensuring that patient care is enhanced by deep, personal bonds among caregivers.

Boys and Girls Club Volunteer

As I tutored a variety of kids at the "Club," I learned to adapt to different learning styles. Two students stood out. One was Sarah, with whom I helped with spelling homework and application of the new words in sentences. Initially, she felt it was acceptable to leave spelling errors within her sentences. However, when I introduced examples of corporate blunders that could have been avoided with a simple spell check, she began to see the real-world importance of her efforts. Recognizing her enjoyment of humor in learning, I made space for jokes and laughter in our lessons. This approach not only improved her academic performance but also enriched our interactions, emphasizing the importance of adaptability and empathy - qualities essential for a physician. I also assisted Diane, an eighth grader with an ambitious goal to become class president. Together, we analyzed how a successful criminal defense lawyer presents a case, gathers evidence, and incites objections. We then drafted a speech that helped her win the election. This experience taught me the effectiveness of connecting school concepts with real-life analogies and examples, enhancing my ability flexibility and adaptability in tutoring different age groups. These skills are akin to those required of a physician, who must adapt their explanations of medical complexities to patients.

Pixorize MCAT Anki Deck

More personal commentary: While studying for the MCAT, I had difficulty that brute forcing facts and making meaningless mneumonics to remember the memorizing amino acids, endocrine pathways, or the ever-so-dreadful metabolic pathways (like the Krebs Cycle). I found the resource Pixorize, which was originally created for medical school board exams, but they branched out to making MCAT videos as well. I would watch their videos and make Anki cards for them, and I found myself being able to recall the amino acids and pathways with ease. I created over 1000 Anki flashcards for all of Pixorize's 200 MCAT videos, and I supplemented the fields of these cards with an annotation of the whole Pixorize image that I made in Powerpoint, to reinforce the memory palace of an MCAT concept. The Anki deck is available for download on Reddit and AnkiHub, and I continue to maintain and update the deck through AnkiHub's syncing features.
